A First-Timer's Guide to Murphys Wine Tasting

📅 June 22, 2026 ⏱ 4 min read 🍷 Wine

Murphys is the wine town that doesn't feel like a wine town. There's no valet parking, no dress code, no $75 tasting flights. It's a one-street Gold Rush village in the Calaveras County foothills where a dozen tasting rooms sit shoulder-to-shoulder in historic storefronts, and you can walk the entire strip in fifteen minutes — wine glass in hand. If you've been meaning to try it but keep putting it off, here's everything you need for your first visit.

Murphys is what Napa was in the 1980s — small-production winemakers who still pour their own wines and remember your name on the second visit.

What Makes Murphys Different from Lodi

Lodi is the 209's big wine region — over 85 tasting rooms spread across rolling vineyard country south of Sacramento. It's a drive-between-wineries experience where you need a car and a plan. Murphys is the opposite. Everything is walkable, concentrated on a single historic Main Street, and the vibe is more mountain town than wine country.

The wines are different too. Lodi excels at old-vine Zinfandel and big warm-climate reds. Murphys sits at higher elevation — 2,000 feet — which gives the grapes cooler nights and a longer growing season. The result is wines with more acidity and complexity: Tempranillo, Barbera, Cabernet Franc, and some surprisingly good Rhone blends that you won't find in the valley.

Walking vs. Driving: Walk. Always Walk.

This is the single best piece of advice for a Murphys first-timer. Park once and walk. The entire tasting room district spans about three blocks on Main Street. You can hit four or five rooms in an afternoon at a comfortable pace with lunch in between, all without moving your car. This is Murphys' superpower — it's the only wine destination in the 209 where a designated driver is truly optional if you pace yourself and walk.

Most rooms charge $10-15 for a flight of five wines, and many waive the fee with a bottle purchase. Plan on spending $50-80 per person for an afternoon of tasting if you buy a bottle or two along the way.

Top 5 Tasting Rooms for First-Timers

1. Newsome Harlow
📍 403 Main St  |  🍷 Known for: Petite Sirah, Zinfandel  |  💰 $10 flight
Start here. The tasting room is tiny and unpretentious, the pours are generous, and the Petite Sirah is one of the best in the Sierra Foothills. Owner Scott Newsome is often behind the bar and will talk wine with you as long as you want. Great education for newcomers without any snobbery.
First-timer tip: Ask about the reserve Petite Sirah if they have any open. It's not always on the standard flight but it's worth requesting.
2. Twisted Oak Winery
📍 363 Main St  |  🍷 Known for: Tempranillo, Spanish varieties  |  💰 $15 flight
Twisted Oak doesn't take itself seriously — the branding is irreverent, the labels are fun — but the wines are genuinely excellent. They specialize in Spanish and Rhone varieties that thrive in the foothill climate. The Tempranillo is a standout, and their Verdelho (white) is a perfect afternoon sipper.
First-timer tip: Grab a glass of the Verdelho and sit on the back patio if weather allows. It's one of the most relaxing spots on Main Street.
3. Ironstone Vineyards
📍 1894 Six Mile Rd (short drive)  |  🍷 Known for: Cabernet, estate wines  |  💰 $15 flight
Technically a short drive from Main Street, but worth it for the grounds alone. Ironstone has a massive estate with gardens, a concert amphitheater, and a 44-pound gold nugget on display in the lobby. The wines are solid and approachable — this is a great first stop if you want the full "wine country experience" before hitting the smaller rooms downtown.
First-timer tip: Visit Ironstone first (before the Main Street rooms) so you can appreciate the contrast between estate winery and boutique tasting room styles.
4. Lavender Ridge Vineyard
📍 425 Main St  |  🍷 Known for: Barbera, Italian varieties  |  💰 $10 flight
A small-production winery focused on Italian grape varieties grown in the Sierra Foothills. The Barbera has beautiful acidity and food-friendliness — it's the kind of wine you'll want to take home a case of for weeknight dinners. The room is intimate and the staff genuinely loves talking about the winemaking process.
First-timer tip: If you like medium-bodied reds that pair well with food (not just sipping wines), this is your spot. Ask about their Sangiovese too.
5. Mineral Wine Bar
📍 419 Main St  |  🍷 Known for: Multi-winery flights, natural wines  |  💰 $12-18 flight
Not a single-winery tasting room but a wine bar that pours selections from multiple Calaveras County producers. Perfect if you want variety without committing to a full flight at each winery. They lean toward natural and minimal-intervention wines and the staff can guide you based on your preferences.
First-timer tip: Tell the bartender what you normally drink and let them surprise you. This is the best place to discover a new winery you'd never find on your own.

Parking Tips

Parking in Murphys is free but limited on weekends. Main Street has angled street parking on both sides — arrive before 11 AM on Saturdays and you'll have no trouble. After noon, circle the block behind Main Street where a small municipal lot handles overflow. Sundays are generally easier. If you're visiting on a holiday weekend or during the Murphys Irish Day celebration in March, park at the community park lot on the east end of town and walk in.

Food Options

You'll want to eat between tastings. Alchemy Market & Cafe is the go-to for sandwiches, cheese boards, and coffee — sit on their back patio. Grounds Restaurant serves upscale comfort food if you want a proper sit-down meal. For quick bites, JoMa's Artisan Ice Cream is a Main Street staple and pairs surprisingly well with a palate reset between reds and whites.

The best wine tasting days include food. Murphys makes it easy — everything is within stumbling distance of a good sandwich.

The Bottom Line

Murphys removes every barrier that makes wine tasting intimidating. No appointments needed at most rooms. No fancy clothes. No winding between vineyards on back roads. Just park, walk, taste, eat, repeat. It's the most approachable wine day trip in the 209, and at 45 minutes from Modesto or an hour from Stockton, it's closer than most people realize. Go on a Thursday if you can — you'll have every tasting bar to yourself.
