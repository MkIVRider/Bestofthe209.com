Summer on the Delta: A Boating Day Trip Guide

📅 June 27, 2026 ⏱ 7 min read 🚤 Boating

There are over 1,000 miles of waterways in the Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta, and on a 100-degree Central Valley summer day, every single one of them looks like paradise. Whether you own a boat, rent one, or just want to kayak through tule islands, the Delta is the 209's greatest summer asset — and most people barely scratch the surface. Here's how to plan the perfect day trip.

The Delta is essentially a 700,000-acre water park that costs nothing to enter. The only catch is you need to know where to go — because most of it looks the same from a map.

Choose Your Launch Point

Where you launch determines what kind of day you'll have. The Delta is massive — you can't see all of it in a day, so pick a zone and own it. Here are the three best starting points for a day trip from anywhere in the 209:

Paradise Point Marina — West Stockton
📍 8095 Rio Blanco Rd, Stockton  |  🅿️ Easy parking  |  ⛽ Fuel dock on-site
The most popular launch for Stockton residents. Full-service marina with fuel, a store, and boat rentals if you don't own. Puts you on the San Joaquin River with access to Fourteen Mile Slough, Disappointment Slough, and the open Delta to the west. Good for skiing, tubing, and cruising.
Best for: Families who want options — you can ski in the main channel, anchor in quiet sloughs for swimming, or cruise to a Delta restaurant for lunch.
Tower Park (Jellystone) — Lodi
📍 14900 W Highway 12, Lodi  |  🛶 Kayak/SUP rentals  |  🍔 On-site restaurant
If you don't have a boat, Tower Park is the move. Kayak and paddleboard rentals put you on the water in minutes. The protected sloughs around the resort are perfect for paddling, and the Waterfront Grille means you don't need to pack lunch. It's the easiest Delta day trip possible.
Best for: Couples and small groups who want a paddle-and-lunch day without the complexity of trailering a boat.
Turner Cut Resort & Marina
📍 12888 W Neugebauer Rd  |  🏕️ RV park on-site  |  ⛽ Fuel dock
A family-owned marina on 20 acres that feels more remote than it is. Launch here for access to the quieter central Delta — less boat traffic, more wildlife, and the kind of winding sloughs that make you forget you're 10 miles from I-5. Great fishing access too.
Best for: People who want peace and quiet. The central Delta channels off Turner Cut feel like a different world from the busy Stockton waterways.

What to Do Once You're on the Water

The Delta offers different experiences depending on what you're after. Here's the breakdown:

Water skiing & wakeboarding: Head for the wider channels — the San Joaquin River mainstem between Stockton and Antioch has the most room. Early morning (before 9 AM) gives you glass-smooth water before the afternoon wind picks up. The stretch near Fourteen Mile Slough is popular for a reason — wide, deep, and relatively uncrowded on weekdays.

Swimming & anchoring: Find a quiet slough with a sandy bank or tule island. Disappointment Slough, White Slough, and the islands around Franks Tract all have spots where you can anchor, drop a swim ladder, and float for hours. Bring a floating mat or tube — the current is gentle in most sloughs.

Cruising & exploring: The Delta rewards those who wander. Pick a direction and follow the channels. You'll pass under drawbridges, alongside hundred-year-old levees, through islands that feel tropical, and past houseboats where people are living their best life. There's no wrong turn — just keep fuel in mind for the return trip.

Kayaking & paddling: The tule-lined sloughs off the main channels are kayak paradise. Otters, herons, turtles, and beavers are all common. Paddle through the narrow cuts between islands where powerboats can't go. The Mokelumne River confluences near Lodi are particularly scenic for paddlers.

The Pack List

Essential Gear

  • Sunscreen (SPF 50+ reapply every 2 hours — water reflects UV)
  • Life jackets for everyone (required by law for under-13 and for skiing)
  • Cooler with ice, water, and drinks (dehydration is real on the Delta)
  • Full fuel tank (gas stations don't exist between marinas)
  • Phone in a waterproof case or dry bag
  • Anchor and 50+ feet of rope (for swimming stops)
  • Shade — bimini top, pop-up canopy, or umbrella
  • Towels (more than you think you need)

Nice to Have

  • Bluetooth speaker (waterproof)
  • Floating mats or inflatable loungers
  • Fishing rod + tackle (you'll see fish breaking the surface)
  • Swim goggles (water clarity varies but can be surprisingly good)
  • Navigation app — Navionics or iBoating shows channels and depths
  • Bug spray (mosquitoes appear at dusk near tules)
  • Change of dry clothes for the drive home

Timing Your Day

The ideal Delta day trip follows this rhythm:

7:00 AM — Launch. Beat the crowd, get the glass-calm water for skiing or photography. The Delta at sunrise is genuinely beautiful — mist on the water, herons fishing, zero boat traffic.

9:00 AM — Cruise to your anchor spot. Find your swimming slough while the water is still calm. The afternoon westerly wind picks up by noon and can make open channels choppy.

10:00 AM – 3:00 PM — Anchor and swim. This is the hot part of the day. Stay in the water, stay in the shade, stay hydrated. This is why you brought a cooler.

3:30 PM — Pull anchor and cruise. The wind usually dies down by 4 PM. Take the scenic route back. Stop at a Delta waterfront restaurant if you're hungry (Wimpy's on Bethel Island, The Point on Brannan Island).

5:00 PM — Back at the ramp. Load up, rinse off at the marina, and you're home by 6 with a sunburn and a good story.

Safety Rules (Non-Negotiable)

The Delta is mostly shallow and slow-moving, but people drown here every year. Keep these non-negotiable:

Wear life jackets. Especially kids. Always. The current doesn't look strong until you're in it.

Don't drink and drive the boat. BUI (boating under the influence) laws are enforced by the Coast Guard and San Joaquin County Sheriff. They're out there on summer weekends.

Watch your depth. The Delta has shallow spots that don't appear obvious. If you don't know the area, stick to marked channels and use a depth finder.

Tell someone your plan. Let someone on shore know where you're launching, your general direction, and when you plan to return. Cell service is spotty in parts of the central Delta.

Respect the current at bridges and narrow cuts. Tidal flow creates strong current at pinch points, especially where channels merge. This is where most accidents happen.

The Delta gives you everything — but it demands respect in return. Know your boat's limits, check the weather, and always have more fuel than you think you need.

After the Water: Where to Eat

You'll be starving, sunburned, and salt-crusted. Here's where to refuel:

Tower Park Waterfront Grille (Lodi side) — Right on the water, casual, good food. You can literally dock your boat and walk in.

Cast Iron Trading Co. (Downtown Stockton) — 20 minutes from Paradise Point. Award-winning burgers and cold craft beer. Perfect post-boat fuel.

Wimpy's Marina (Bethel Island) — Classic Delta dive with burgers, beers, and a deck overlooking the water. The kind of place where everyone is in flip-flops and nobody cares what you look like.

Rent vs. Own

Don't own a boat? No problem. The Delta has multiple rental options:

Paradise Point Marina rents ski boats, pontoons, patio boats, and fishing boats. Full day rentals typically run $300-600 depending on vessel size.

Tower Park rents kayaks ($60/4 hours single, $75 double) and pontoon boats for larger groups.

Kokopelli Kayak Rentals offers guided wildlife kayak tours through the tules — a completely different Delta experience that doesn't require boat knowledge.

First-timers should start with a pontoon rental — they're nearly impossible to mess up, hold 8-12 people comfortably, and give you the full Delta experience without the stress of trailering a ski boat.
